Work

UX/UI Case Study

Project Duration: 10 months

Moon is a responsive digital wallet app that allows users to store multiple financial accounts in one place, customize budgets, schedule transactions, and view expenses reports. The app acts as a secure bank account and transactional card whenever, wherever, making it 'your wallet on the go'.

Problem Statement

Users find themselves shopping a lot more online than usual as social restrictions due to the pandemic ceased them from enjoying the liberty to go out, hang out with family and friends, and do any kinds of activities. As a result, users' financial health ends up being out of shape as they could not control their shopping habits and overspend.

Solution

A digital solution that can track users' spending to keep them informed of their monthly savings and help them determine what they need to work on to reach their financial goals. Budget customization is one of the many features that can help create good money habits, have a solid financial plan, and reach savings goals.

Design Process

Competitive Analyses

As part of the first key steps in planning a successful design, I conducted a competitive analysis of the two main competitors - GooglePay and PayPal - to give me an insight into what might resonate with users in the market.

User Research

The two methods of user research - Surveys and Interviews - were conducted to collect qualitative and quantitative data. Survey method was used to gain initial insights from users and interview method for a more in depth research.

  • To gain insights into users’ behaviours with existing digital wallet apps.
  • To determine the users’ purpose of utilizing a digital wallet app.
  • To better understand the users’ deciding factors when choosing digital wallet apps.

Affinity Mapping

The stage where I analyze users' needs and pain points by sorting and mapping out data collected from the user surveys and interviews. I used these data to find out users' behaviors/attitudes, needs/goals, frustrations, and more.

Insights

  • Some users use different e-wallet apps for different things. Since every user behaves differently, a way to tailor their needs is to allow for customization. Users will more likely to use an app if it serves them specific purposes.
  • A simple UI can help speed up every transaction, which can be a major factor for using and staying with our app.
  • A multi-layer security when using an e-wallet app will transmit the feeling of security to users.
  • Transparency in data use is key to turn users’ concerns into an opportunity to build trust in our app.
  • Users prefer to see a categorized overview of their expenses. As this seems to be an important feature to users, we can focus on accurately classifying categories rather than having a general list of categories.
  • Though most users are not comfortable storing all cards in one place, there are opportunities to convince them to use our app; e.g., advantages of not having to log in to different apps to access different bank cards, providing an overview of all their money across multiple accounts, eliminating transaction fees, and more.

Card Sorting

I used the card sorting method to better design my information architecture. This method helped me organized the app's information and make it more intuitive. The digital test was conducted through OptimalSort with 8 participants.

Analysis

  • There are three main clusters (green box) found to have related pairings – information from boxes #1 and #3 can be seen under ‘Account’ in my current Sitemap below Tier 2, while #2 shows a combined information from ‘Budget’, ‘Reports’ and ‘Transactions’.
  • Information found on #1 and #3 can potentially be grouped into one as there is an apparent interrelation here (yellow lines). Hence, validating my assumption in terms of my site structure in this particular area.
  • Generally, I believe that most of my assumptions in terms of my site structure were validated by majority of the participants during this Open Card Sort exercise. Change/ adding of wordings or names might also do good.

Low- to Mid-Fidelity Prototyping

To start fleshing out interaction ideas and forming a flow to my prototypes, I did hand sketching. This method helped me to really focus on the high-level functionality of the app while not pondering on other details like spacing and colors.

High-Fidelity Prototyping

During this phase in the project, I went ahead and added color to the app's screens, added UI elements and worked on some alignments, too. Below are images of how some of the app's screens evolved over time after usability testings and multiple iterations.

Reducing cognitive overload...

Despite the heavy-data content, I made sure that every element in the screen serve a clear purpose so to not diminish the visibility of the relevant units of information.

Making use of the space...

To improve the visuals, filling in negative spaces in the design was one of the solutions. This information architecture will provide users with the necessary information within one screen and not to overlook any important ones.

Eliminating unnecessary steps...

In order to shortened the lengthy sign up process, I omitted some input fields for a quick and easy flow. Labels and assistive icons were also added to tell users of what information is to be entered.

Testing

The clickable prototype was developed and used for the evaluation of the app's usability. There were a total of 7 participants - 6 moderated remote tests and 1 moderated in-person test. Jakob Nielsen's severity rating scale was used to measure error severity of all gathered data, a Single Ease Question (SEQ) 7-point rating scale was also utilized to quantify the level of difficulty in performing each task. Findings were laid out on a Rainbow Spreadsheet and identified 4 major issues.

Reflection

Hard work pays off. Indeed is this true to those who are ambitious, determined and fearless individual. Some people would say don't look back, but there is a saying wrote by Steve Jobs that goes "You can't connect the dots looking forward; you can only connect them looking backwards." How I got to this point is all because of the past experiences and realizations I had in life and there is no way I can, should or must forget those. I consider this UX design project as an award despite all the challenges along the way. At the end of the day, I know I delivered. Working on this project made me appreciate having worked in the hospitality industry even more. In UX design, it is vital to anticipate users' needs. Providing them what they need even before them knowing it will generally set a positive user experience.

Building this product for the first time, I learned a number of things. To mention a few, I learned:

  • design is never done. Designing is a continuous process, especially if we want to keep up with the trend.
  • expect mistakes and be open to change. I like the idea of constantly receiving new ideas. Implementing changes is only a second step.
  • the simpler the better. Build an experience that feels intuitive. I believe it is human nature that we know what we know. But we are not perfect. As a designer, making things easy and simple is always a way to go.
  • consistency is key. It is so important that, as a designer, we try to reduce cognitive overload in order to avoid frustrations. This not only maintain a good user experience, but it will also add to the simplicity of the design.

Typography and Color Palettes

For my home screen, the font style Lulo Clean is used for the header, while Avenir Light is used for the title to create visual diversity as well as graphic balance. The rest of the pages – Case Study, About, and Contact – uses Playfair Display for the header and Montserrat for paragraph or body to create a good combination that optimizes readability and gives a modern, clean feel to my portfolio.

The main colors for my website are deep jungle green, a shade of orange-yellow (Crayola), and white. According to a study, deep jungle green can mean being clean, elegant, and accessible. These are all part of my criteria and I want to use colors that are rich and versatile, but not overly colorful. Orange-yellow, on the other hand, is normally associated with feelings of warmth, friendliness, and creativity, which also reflects my personality, and complement perfectly with the deep jungle green.
